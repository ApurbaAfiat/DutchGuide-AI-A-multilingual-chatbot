# Healthcare & Health Insurance in the Netherlands

## Overview
The Netherlands has one of the world's best healthcare systems, consistently ranked in the top 5 in Europe. Healthcare is organised around:
- **General practitioners (huisartsen)** — Your first point of contact for almost all health issues
- **Specialists (specialisten)** — Only accessible via GP referral
- **Emergency services** — For life-threatening situations

## Health Insurance (Zorgverzekering)

### Is Health Insurance Mandatory?
**Yes.** Everyone who lives or works in the Netherlands (including international students and workers) is legally required to have Dutch health insurance within 4 months of registering at the gemeente. Failure to get insurance results in fines from the CAK (Central Administrative Office).

### Basic Package (Basisverzekering)
All Dutch health insurance providers must offer the same basic package by law:
- GP visits (free)
- Hospital treatment
- Emergency care
- Prescription medicines (within formulary)
- Mental health care (basic)
- Maternity care

The basic package costs approximately **€130–180/month** (2024, depending on provider).

### Deductible (Eigen Risico)
- Mandatory deductible: **€385/year** (2024)
- You pay the first €385 of healthcare costs yourself (GP visits are excluded)
- Higher voluntary deductible = lower monthly premium

### Healthcare Allowance (Zorgtoeslag)
If your income is below a certain threshold, the Dutch government provides a healthcare allowance to help pay for insurance:
- Apply via the Belastingdienst (Tax Authority) using your DigiD
- Can reduce your effective premium by €100–120/month
- Students with little/no income often qualify

### International Student Health Insurance
- If you're an EU citizen, your EHIC/European Health Insurance Card provides temporary coverage — but you still need Dutch insurance for long-term stays
- Non-EU students on a residence permit must have Dutch health insurance
- Some universities offer group insurance packages — check with your university

## The GP System (Huisarts)

### Registering with a GP
1. Find a GP accepting new patients near your address (use Google Maps or zoekeehuisarts.nl)
2. Contact the practice and ask to register (inschrijven)
3. Bring your BSN, ID, and health insurance details
4. You are now registered with that practice

### When to See a GP
- Non-emergency health issues (cold, flu, infections, injuries, mental health)
- Referrals to specialists
- Prescription medicines
- Preventive care

### GP Consultation
- GP visits are FREE (covered by your basic insurance, not subject to the deductible)
- Appointments are usually 10 minutes — be concise and specific about your symptoms
- You can also call the practice's advice line (triagiste) to ask whether you need to come in

### Out-of-Hours GP (Huisartsenpost)
- For urgent but non-life-threatening issues outside office hours
- Call 0900-1010 (not 112) to speak with a triage nurse
- If needed, they direct you to the nearest huisartsenpost

## Pharmacies (Apotheken)
- Require a prescription for most medicines
- Over-the-counter: Paracetamol, ibuprofen, antihistamines available from pharmacies and supermarkets
- Each neighbourhood has a "duty pharmacy" (dienstdoende apotheek) for after-hours emergencies

## Mental Health
- Mental health services in the Netherlands are thorough but have long waiting lists
- GP can refer you to a mental health professional (psychologist/psychiatrist)
- University health services often offer free counselling for students
- Anonymous online support: ggzstandaarden.nl, letsconnect.nl (free for students)

## Dental Care (Tandarts)
- Dental care for adults is NOT covered by the basic insurance
- Children under 18 have dental care covered
- Adults should get supplemental dental insurance (aanvullende verzekering)
- Annual dental check-up: ~€40–80
- Filling: ~€70–120

## Tips for New Arrivals
1. Register with a GP in week 1 — practices fill up fast
2. Get health insurance within your first week (mandatory within 4 months)
3. Keep your insurance card and BSN accessible for any medical visit
4. In emergencies, call 112 — all operators speak English
